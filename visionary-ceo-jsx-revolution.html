How Alex Wilcox Built JSX and Redefined Regional Flying

📅 June 2025
✎ Alex Wilcox
⌚ 7 min read

In 2016, Alex Wilcox looked at the regional air travel market and saw exactly what everyone else had already dismissed: an industry-wide failure of imagination. Short-haul flying had become the least pleasant segment of American aviation, and almost no one in a position to change it seemed bothered. Wilcox was bothered. He founded JSX — originally JetSuiteX — to prove that the hop between Dallas and Houston, or Phoenix and Las Vegas, could feel like a completely different product.

The premise was deceptively simple. If the worst part of flying short distances is everything that happens before you board — the security lines, the gate crowding, the interminable waiting — then remove those elements. JSX operates from private terminals or dedicated FBOs, passengers arrive 20 minutes before departure, and the boarding process takes minutes, not an hour. The aircraft are right-sized for the routes. The experience, from arrival to wheels up, has been stripped of the friction that makes commercial aviation feel like a civic ordeal.

Why the Market Was Ready

Alex Wilcox's timing was not accidental. By the mid-2010s, legacy carriers had largely ceded the premium short-haul segment to whoever wanted to serve it. The major airlines had consolidated aggressively, retrenched into their hub-and-spoke dominance, and — for the most part — stopped competing on passenger experience on routes under two hours. Business travelers were driving or taking Amtrak because the airport experience had become a net negative worth avoiding.

Wilcox had spent enough years watching passenger behavior to understand that time is the scarcest resource for the traveler most likely to pay a premium. The JSX model attacked the time problem directly. His career spanning three decades in aviation — from Virgin Atlantic to JetBlue to Kingfisher — had given him a comprehensive map of where airlines routinely failed their customers and why. JSX was the synthesis of those observations.

"JSX has flown hundreds of thousands of customers on tens of thousands of flights while consistently maintaining an industry-leading Net Promoter Score of 85 or above."

The Numbers That Tell the Story

For anyone who suspects the JSX model is a niche product for a narrow slice of the market, the numbers say otherwise. Since its founding, the carrier has completed tens of thousands of flights and served hundreds of thousands of passengers. Its Net Promoter Score — the standard measure of whether customers like a product enough to recommend it — has stayed at 85 or above since launch. To put that in context: most major US airlines operate with NPS scores in the 30s and 40s. Banks and cable companies tend to score in the negative. An NPS of 85 is the territory of Apple and USAA.

That score is a consequence of the product Alex Wilcox designed, not an accident of a lucky demographic. When you remove the friction from the airport experience and consistently deliver on the promise, passengers respond with the strongest signal in the business: they come back, and they tell others. The customer experience philosophy behind JSX is the engine that drives those numbers — and it stems directly from Wilcox's insistence that the traveler's time and comfort are not secondary considerations.

Disruption as a Design Principle

What makes Alex Wilcox distinctive among airline executives is that his instinct for disruption doesn't come from a consulting framework or a business school case study. It comes from decades of observing, at close range, what passengers actually want — and noticing the gap between that and what most carriers provide. At JetBlue, the disruption was proving that a low-cost carrier could offer leather seats and live television without sacrificing the fare equation. At JSX, the disruption is proving that a regional carrier can offer a private-aviation-adjacent experience at prices that make commercial sense.

The common thread is the willingness to ask an uncomfortable question: if this product were designed from scratch today, with the customer's experience as the only constraint, what would it look like? That question produced JetBlue in 1999. It produced JSX in 2016. And it continues to drive the decisions Wilcox makes as he expands the carrier's network from its Dallas base across the country. His recognition as a Henry Crown Fellow by the Aspen Institute reflects a reputation that extends well beyond the airline sector.

What Comes Next

JSX is not finished. The regional air travel opportunity is large, and the competitive dynamics that made the model viable in 2016 have not changed in the carriers' favor. Legacy airlines continue to consolidate, reduce service on thinner routes, and face structural headwinds around labor costs and aging fleets. The premium short-haul passenger remains underserved by the mainstream market.

Wilcox has positioned JSX to fill that gap at scale. The carrier's expansion of routes, its continued focus on FBO-style terminal access, and its discipline around the product experience suggest an operation that understands its own value proposition clearly. For Alex Wilcox in Dallas, the JSX revolution is still in progress — and the traveler who has experienced it once tends to agree.

Conclusion

The story of JSX is ultimately a story about what happens when an experienced executive refuses to accept the limitations of an existing industry. Alex Wilcox spent thirty years watching aviation do things the same way because they had always been done that way, and he spent that time cataloguing every unnecessary friction point in the passenger journey. JSX is the result: a carrier that didn't just improve on the regional flying experience but fundamentally reimagined it. That is what visionary leadership looks like in practice.
