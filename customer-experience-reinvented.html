Customer Experience Reinvented: The JSX Standard

📅 May 2025
✎ Alex Wilcox
⌚ 6 min read

Ask most airline CEOs what their top priority is, and they'll tell you — in various formulations — that it's the customer. Ask their operational teams why flights are delayed, bags are lost, and boarding sequences resemble organized confusion, and you'll quickly learn how far that stated priority is from the actual one. Alex Wilcox built JSX on the premise that the gap between what airlines say about customer experience and what they actually deliver could be turned into a competitive weapon.

The JSX model is not a loyalty points scheme or a refurbished lounge. It is a structural reimagining of the entire flying experience, starting from the moment a passenger decides to travel and ending when they walk out the other side. Alex Wilcox in Dallas designed a product where the friction that makes commercial aviation unpleasant — the security theater, the gate crowds, the middle seats, the indifferent crew — has been systematically removed. What's left is the part of flying that was never the problem: getting from one city to another quickly.

The Friction Audit

Before JSX could improve the passenger experience, Wilcox had to map exactly where the experience went wrong. The answer, drawn from his three decades in aviation, was comprehensive. The friction wasn't in the flight itself — it was in everything surrounding it. Check-in queues. Security lines that swallowed 45 minutes of a two-hour trip. Gate areas designed for warehousing passengers rather than providing comfort. Boarding processes that turned 150 people into a slow-motion rugby scrum. Bags that might or might not be where you expected them at the other end.

JSX's answer was to operate out of private terminals and FBOs — the fixed base operators used by private aviation — where those friction points simply don't exist. There's no TSA line because the aircraft capacity and operational context don't require it. There's no gate crowd because the terminal isn't shared with 2,000 other passengers. You arrive 20 minutes before departure, walk to the aircraft, and board. The entire pre-flight experience takes less time than the average TSA queue at a major hub.

The NPS Story

Net Promoter Score — the metric that measures how likely customers are to recommend a product — is the most direct evidence of whether the JSX model works. JSX has sustained an NPS of 85 or above since its founding. That number deserves context. The average US airline NPS hovers in the 30s and 40s. Southwest, long considered the most customer-friendly of the legacy carriers, typically scores in the 60s on a good year. An NPS of 85 is not an airline score — it's a luxury consumer brand score.

The implications are significant. A high NPS doesn't just reflect happy customers; it reflects customers who are actively marketing the product to their networks. Every JSX passenger who scores a 9 or 10 is a sales channel. They are telling colleagues, recommending routes to their teams, and converting skeptics who had written off short-haul flying as not worth the trouble. That organic referral engine, built on genuine product satisfaction, is what has allowed JSX to grow without the marketing budgets that major carriers deploy. What JSX Learned from Wilcox's Earlier Career

The customer experience philosophy at JSX didn't emerge fully formed in 2016. It is the distillation of lessons Alex Wilcox carried from every role in his career. At Virgin Atlantic, he saw that a premium flying experience wasn't exclusive to business class — that the quality of interaction between crew and passenger mattered at every price point. At JetBlue, he helped demonstrate that a low-cost carrier could offer comfort features — leather seating, live television — that customers hadn't expected and immediately valued. Those early lessons shaped a consistent hypothesis: passengers respond powerfully when you give them more than they expected, and they punish you equally powerfully when you give them less.

JSX is essentially the realization of that hypothesis at scale. The carrier's ongoing public conversation led by Wilcox on social media reflects an executive who thinks about the passenger relationship constantly, not just in quarterly strategy reviews. The transparency and accessibility that characterize his public presence are an extension of the same philosophy that drives the JSX product.

The Scalability Question

Critics of premium experience models often raise the scalability question: can a product built around intimacy and attention to detail survive growth? The JSX model has been tested on this question, and so far the answer is yes — because the friction removal that defines the JSX experience is structural rather than dependent on high staff-to-passenger ratios. The 20-minute arrival window and FBO access work as well for a full aircraft as they do for a half-full one. The boarding process is fast because the terminal infrastructure makes it fast, not because there are more employees per passenger.

What the model does require is operational discipline and leadership that is unwilling to trade the experience for a short-term cost reduction. That commitment comes from the top. Alex Wilcox has built an organization in Dallas that treats the passenger experience as the constraint that other decisions must satisfy — not as a variable to be optimized away when margins get tight. His career trajectory and the consistency of JSX's NPS suggest that discipline is durable.

Conclusion

The JSX standard for customer experience is not an aspiration — it's a measurement. A Net Promoter Score of 85, sustained over years and tens of thousands of flights, is the clearest available evidence that Alex Wilcox's conviction about the passenger experience is correct. The gap between what airlines say about serving customers and what JSX actually does is measurable in points, referrals, and repeat bookings. That gap is also a business model.
